I've developed an experiment design and a small, preliminary experiment for a project idea proposed by the conceptual reasoning team at Redwood Research. The full proposal and design are linked in the repo.

We are rapidly entering a regime where AI research will be partially or even fully automated by LLMs. While capabilities work usually has verifiable reward, we may expect alignment research to have many difficult conceptual problems without a verifiable ground truth, so we cannot just reward the model for being correct. A workaround is to train models to be more consistent. Consider a claim A, for instance are insects conscious. We cannot verify it, but we can ask the model for P(A), and then for P(A and B), where B is the claim that insects have moral status. By the laws of probability P(A and B) should be at most P(A), yet LLMs violate constraints like this all the time. The hope is that training a model to stop contradicting itself pushes it toward a more integrated world model, and that better reasoning comes along with it. Before building all of that, I wanted to test the claim my draft leaned on: that consistency training on equivalent phrasings would be equivalent to training against a more powerful reward model.

First, I came up with the approach and wrote up the design, including the reward and the different kinds of inconsistency to train on. Second, I built a small experiment for the phrasing case, the one I was most excited about. I take a claim, ask it several equivalent ways, have a small model answer each many times, and average to get a target T; the reward pushes each answer toward T. Instead of training, I tested the key assumption directly: I had a much stronger model, Claude Opus, read pairs of answers blind and pick which one reasoned better, then checked whether it agreed with the consistency reward.

What surprised me is that, unlike what the team at Redwood found, I got a null result. The answer closest to T was preferred only about half the time, no better than a coin flip, and this held for both a 4B model and a bigger reasoning model. Reading the grader's reasons made it clear why: T is just the middle of the model's own guesses, so it carries the model's own mistakes. When the model is confidently wrong as a group, the best answer is the odd one out, which is exactly the answer this reward punishes.

In terms of mistakes, I started with contested ethics questions, a bad call: there, two answers can differ because they take different but reasonable views, not because one reasons better, so consistency had no real right direction to point toward. I also botched the conjunction questions twice, once making them so obvious that the model never slipped, once rewording them until they no longer meant the same thing.

Some things are still unresolved. A model can be perfectly consistent and confidently wrong, so this is just one aspect of conceptual reasoning. The open question is whether the reward works on questions with a real answer, where better reasoning points the same way and the model is right often enough that T lands near the truth. And the real purpose of the project, whether internal consistency results in better conceptual reasoning, is still untested.
